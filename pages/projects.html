Design for Defense - National Geospatial Intelligence Agency (NGA)

This five-person interdisciplinary team endeavor derived from a problem posed to us by the NGA in an entrepreneurial setting. The ultimate goal was to produce dual-purpose (military and private-sector) entity use-cases. In short, the NGA wanted us to develop a software solution to identify salient changes in mass quantities of geoJSON data and transform it into actionable information.

This data came from the European Space Agency's (ESA) Sentienel-1 mission. The mission is comprised of a constellation of two polar-orbiting C-band synthetic aperture radar (SAR) satellites. The data we were given was preprocessed by the NGA's internal change-detection algorithm. What we recieved was a 700+ GB of geoJSON and KMZ files.

We worked as a team to develop two code-backed approaches to handing the Sentienel-1 SAR data, namely heatmapping and clustering. However, based on the low resolution of Sentienel-1 data compared to emerging technologies, which offer a magnitude increase in resolution, it was a tough case to sell for private-sector use.

Therefore, we gave the NGA our code and a thoughtful detailed write-up that explained the problem and landscape as we came to know it. This lead to our team to a major pivot to focus on wildfire mitigation. We landed on this focus largely due to the fact that SAR data is unique in its ability acquire imagery regardless of the weather. In order to make a case for an entrepreneurial endeavor, we conducted interviews, market research, and establied industry partners. The video on this page stands as a testament to the deep dive we took into this realm.

Senior Design Capstone - U.S. Army Research Office (ARO)

In place of the traditional Computer Science Capstone project, I applied for CU Boulder's Capstone Exchange program. I wanted to work on an interdisciplinary team in the Mechanical Engineering Capstone, called Senior Design. After I was in the program, I then applied and was accepted for this specific project.

The team was comprised of seven Mechanical Engineering students, and myself being the sole Computer Science student. We were given a prompt by the ARO titled "Autonomous device/system for ground-based Warfighter protection, supply,and/or evacuation". This amounted to a largely open-ended design and prototyping project. We spent the entire Fall 2019 semester developing the scope, design, manufacturing, assembly, and test plans for the project. This included drawing up industry standard documents and presentations such as the Critical Design Review. The lionshare of the team's time during Spring 2020 semester was spent putting together and testing our device.

My role as the team Computer Scientist was broadly split in two. Firstly, I was to research, pitch, make diagrams, and field questions during the iterative design process to aid in determining scope and feasibility. Secondly I was to taking the lead with computer and electronics hardware installation, software implementation, and testing.

Part of this process included using Python's Computer Vision module OpenCV with a webcam attached to our device. This was to autonomously detect some visual target, find its location, and deploy defensive system in response. In order to accomplish this, we used a Raspberry Pi with Raspian (Debian-based operating system) as the computer for the electronic components interface with. The webcam was plugged into the Raspberry Pi's USB port, while the rest of the components were connected via the general purpose input/output ports. Bebop Parrot 2 Path Planner

This robotics project was a four-person team effort during the Fall 2019 semester. We decided to write a color-coded path planner via blob detection for a quadcopter. My contributions entailed installing and managing the ROS middleware on an Ubunutu Linux virtual machine and aquiring the required Bebop packages. I setup the initial quadcopter communications, which were basic controlling movement via bash commands. I then wrote the same type of commands in Python and merged it with the groups blob-detection code. We then worked as a group to bring together and test the color-detection and motor controlling. PickUp/Pindown App

This project began as a six-person team in the Spring of 2019 in Software Development Methods and Tools class. The team designed and implemented a working Android app prototype named PickUp. The name is from the common term for an impromptu sports game, which was the types of events we were targeting. The app was created using Google's Flutter SDK with a Firebase database and Google Cloud Platform for user authentication. My chief contributions to the app protoype were establishing the authentication and required API keys as well as connecting Firebase to the frontend. The team used weekly Scrum meetings and a broader interative Agile methodology throughout the semester. The team was successful in developing a protoype version found here that allowed users to create an account, securely login, and post and remove events at predetermined locations on a map.

This project gave rise to efforts to rebrand and build a new start-up around map-based events app, called Pindown. This startup went on to grow and pitch at the 2020 New Venture Challenge. It has since dissolved. Below is some images from PickUp that were remade into wireframes for Pindown.
